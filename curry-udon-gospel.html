The Gospel of Curry Udon:

Starting from a mild skepticism toward gluten-free diets, this paper arrives — via the Kojiki, Buddhism, and an Edo-period peasant uprising — at the fundamental truth of the universe.

Abstract / 要旨

This paper demonstrates that Curry Udon (カレーうどん) is not merely a food, but the inevitable apex of Japanese religious syncretism. We trace a logical chain from gut health science through Shinto cosmogony, Buddhist spice culture, the pilgrimage economy of Shikoku, and the spontaneous mass ecstasy of the Edo period, arriving at the inescapable conclusion: Curry Udon is sacred. Its splashing is divine will. Resistance is heresy.

Chapter I

グルテンフリーという冒涜

Gluten-Free as Blasphemy

腸活のついでにグルテンフリーを実践する者は多い。しかし問うべきである——香川県民を見よ。

Many practice gluten-free diets alongside gut health routines. But consider: the citizens of Kagawa Prefecture consume udon at a rate that staggers the imagination. If gluten were universally harmful, Kagawa would be a prefecture of invalids. It is not. Ergo:

グルテンフリーを推奨する者は、神の意思に逆らっている可能性がある。
Those who promote gluten-free diets may be acting against divine will.

Note: scientific evidence for gluten-free benefits is robust for those with celiac disease or NCGS. For everyone else, the evidence is, to put it charitably, thin. Kagawa Prefecture has been conducting the control group experiment for centuries.

Chapter II

香川県民は神の隣人である

The People of Kagawa Are Neighbors of the Gods

According to the Kojiki (古事記), Japan's oldest chronicle, the divine couple Izanagi and Izanami first created Awajishima (淡路島). Shikoku — home of Kagawa Prefecture — was the third island. The people of Kagawa have lived next door to the birthplace of creation since the beginning of time.

Awajishima is also Japan's foremost producer of onions (玉ねぎ). Onions are the foundation of curry's sweetness. This is not a coincidence. This is cosmological planning.

Chapter III

食の神仏習合：カレーうどん大統一理論

Culinary Syncretism: The Grand Unified Theory

Buddhism originated in the Indian subcontinent — the same land that gave the world curry spices. Both traveled the Silk Road eastward. Both arrived in Japan. Japan, uniquely, fused them with its native Shinto tradition into Shinbutsu-shūgō (神仏習合). The logical continuation of this process is self-evident:

The Meiji government's Shinbutsu Bunri (神仏分離) decree of 1868 attempted to separate Shinto and Buddhism. Curry Udon ignored this entirely and has remained a syncretic masterpiece to this day.

Chapter IV

お遍路黄衣化改革論

On the Necessary Reform of Pilgrimage Vestments

The Shikoku Pilgrimage (四国八十八箇所) is a masterpiece of systems design: 88 sacred sites, distributed economic stimulus, the psychological support of "Dōgyō ninin" (同行二人 — "two traveling together," meaning one is always with Kōbō Daishi), and gamified completion mechanics 1,200 years before gamification was a word.

However: pilgrims wear white (白衣). This is a critical design flaw.

「カレーうどんは跳ねるものなり。抗うことなかれ。白き衣を着て挑む者は、己の未熟を知れ。」
"Curry Udon shall splash. Resist it not. Those who wear white and dare approach have only themselves to blame." — Kare Daimyōjin (カレー大明神)

The case for yellow vestments (黄衣) is overwhelming: turmeric's natural antimicrobial properties, superior visibility on mountain trails, and — critically — total immunity to curry udon splash events. Kūkai would absolutely have chosen yellow had curry udon existed in the 9th century. This is simply a 1,200-year-old patch that needs applying.

Chapter V

ええじゃないか ——民衆の悟りと汁跳ねの受容

Ee Ja Naika: Peasant Enlightenment and the Acceptance of Splash

In 1867, as the Edo period collapsed, a spontaneous mass movement swept Japan. People danced in the streets chanting "Ee ja naika!" (ええじゃないか — "Ain't it fine? / Whatever!"). Its origins were mysterious. Its purpose was unclear. It spread virally, which is to say: it was Japan's first meme.

What does this have to do with curry udon? Everything. When the broth hits your white shirt, there are two responses available to the human spirit:

① Lament. ② ええじゃないか。

The enlightened practitioner chooses ②. This is the fusion of resignation (諦め) and festival spirit (祭り心) — the highest psychological state available to the curry udon enthusiast.

Chapter VI

スパイスと線香の同一起源仮説

The Common Origin of Spice and Incense

Ancient Indian religious ceremonies burned spices as offerings. The aromatic smoke was communication with the divine. This practice traveled the same Silk Road routes as Buddhism, arriving in Japan where it bifurcated into:

① 線香 (Senkō) — incense, burned at temples and on the Shikoku pilgrimage
② スパイス — curry spices, consumed in curry udon

Both are, fundamentally, the same thing: fragrant matter from India, used to reach a higher state of being. The pilgrimage path smells of incense. The kitchen smells of curry. These are not different journeys.
